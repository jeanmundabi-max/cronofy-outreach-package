Cronofy — Growth Diagnosis
Prepared for Octave Auger, Head of Marketing

Cronofy Has the Product. Nylas Has the Visibility.

The scheduling Application Programming Interface market is being won in the demand generation layer, not the product layer. And right now, Cronofy is not competing in that layer.

The Core Leak

You have scheduling infrastructure that powers Wise, Teamtailor, and Docplanner. You have a 4.8-star rating. Your Application Programming Interface outperforms Nylas on compliance, stability, and cross-domain queries. You just closed £15 million from BGF with a mandate to grow.

But Nylas is spending $175 million on paid acquisition, developer content at 10-plus articles per month, and a structured Account Based Marketing program. They are visible to every developer who searches "scheduling Application Programming Interface" or "Nylas alternative" right now. Cronofy is not.

The result: your best potential customers — large Applicant Tracking System vendors, healthcare platforms, professional services software companies — are finding Nylas first. Not because Nylas is better. Because Nylas shows up and Cronofy does not yet have the demand generation motion to compete in that channel.

Product-led growth got you to 180,000 organisations. The enterprise tier above that requires a different approach entirely.
What This Seems to Indicate

The existing growth model has a ceiling it has not hit yet — but is about to

Product-led growth works when buyers are already looking. It does not create demand among enterprise buyers who are not yet evaluating scheduling Application Programming Interfaces. Teamtailor found Cronofy. The next 50 Applicant Tracking System vendors will not find you the same way.

The £15 million from BGF has created a timing pressure the current team cannot absorb alone

You have 6 people in marketing, no demand generation person in seat, and a board expecting enterprise growth. A new Demand Generation Manager will spend their first 60 days building playbooks from scratch — not running campaigns — unless a foundation already exists.

Cronofy is winning on product but losing at the point of evaluation

When a product manager evaluates calendar Application Programming Interfaces, they look at documentation, developer community, comparison articles, and paid results. Nylas has all four. Cronofy has strong documentation but no developer community and no paid presence on the terms that matter.

The enterprise tier is reachable but untouched

There are 200-plus Applicant Tracking System platforms globally. Cronofy's customers — Indeed, Teamtailor, Greenhouse — prove the model works at scale. But there is no Account Based Marketing motion identifying the next 50 platforms that should be customers. The accounts exist. The outreach does not.

Paid acquisition is starting from zero at the wrong moment

Cal.com raised $25 million and is building developer community and content. Nylas is running paid across every high-intent keyword. Every month without paid acquisition is a month both competitors consolidate mindshare that Cronofy will have to buy back later at a higher cost.

The Fibbler intent signal fires — and then nothing happens

The Demand Generation Manager job description lists Fibbler as a tool already in the stack. Intent data tells you which accounts are researching scheduling Application Programming Interfaces. But without an outbound sequence following up within 24 hours, that signal sits in a dashboard. Warm accounts cool off unanswered.

Enterprise Applicant Tracking System vendors at the scale of Greenhouse or SmartRecruiters represent $30,000 to $100,000 per year — often across 3 to 5 year terms.

The United States market — where 60% of Cronofy's revenue comes from — requires proactive outreach, not passive discovery. United States enterprise buyers do not browse integration marketplaces. They get reached. Standing still in that market is not neutral. It is conceding ground.

What I Would Fix First
1

Build the Account Based Marketing target list now

A list of 50 Applicant Tracking System vendors and healthcare platforms — with decision-makers identified, Fibbler intent signals tagged, and accounts prioritised by tier — should exist before any campaign launches. The first 30 days of demand generation should be spent executing, not researching. This is the infrastructure that makes every other motion faster.

Unlocks: the Demand Generation Manager runs campaigns from day one instead of building the foundation.
2

Activate paid acquisition on the three highest-intent terms now

"Scheduling Application Programming Interface", "calendar Application Programming Interface for developers", and "Nylas alternative" are terms developers search when they are 30 to 60 days from a purchase decision. Every week without paid presence on these terms sends those leads to Nylas by default.

Unlocks: inbound from developers who are evaluating right now, not after a 60-day ramp.
3

Separate the developer acquisition track from the enterprise buyer track

A developer self-serving on the Emerging plan at $819 per month is a different buyer from the product director at a 200-person Applicant Tracking System company evaluating a Strategic contract. One content strategy means neither converts well. Developer buyers need comparison content and community. Enterprise buyers need account-level targeting and case-study-led outreach.

Unlocks: higher conversion at both ends. No more messaging that is too technical for buyers and too commercial for developers.
4

Connect Fibbler signals to a live outbound sequence

Account-level intent should trigger an outbound sequence within 24 hours of a signal firing. Building the connection between Fibbler, HubSpot, and a three-step sequence takes about two weeks. It does not require the Demand Generation Manager to be in seat.

Unlocks: warm intent signals convert into conversations instead of going cold in a dashboard.
The Shift

Right now, Cronofy's enterprise growth depends on who happens to find the product. The Teamtailor and Docplanner wins prove the model works — but they were not systematically generated. The next stage is not working harder with the same model. It is adding a structured layer on top of product-led growth: Account Based Marketing to the accounts that should be customers, paid acquisition to capture developers who are evaluating now, and intent signals that trigger outreach rather than sit in a dashboard. That shift — from passive discovery to active enterprise capture — is what the BGF investment was designed to fund. The question is how fast it gets built.

Which of these feels like the most urgent gap from your side right now — the Account Based Marketing target list, the paid acquisition setup, or getting the Fibbler signals connected to outbound?
